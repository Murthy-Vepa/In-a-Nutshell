    üìñ Agile Introduction

    intro overview

    Definition

    Agile is an iterative way of managing projects and developing software that makes it easier for teams to deliver value to their customers more quickly and effectively.

    An agile team is to deliver small but consumable increments of work rather than wagering everything on a "big bang" launch.

    Agile: Incremental + Iterative
    Agile emphasizes delivering work in small, iterative cycles that allow for continuous feedback and adaptation.

    Agile Methodology

    Agile methodology is a project management approach that prioritizes cross-functional collaboration and continuous improvement. It divides projects into smaller phases and guides teams through cycles of planning, execution, and evaluation.

    Key Benefits of Agile

    ‚ö° Faster Time to Market

    Deliver value incrementally and get products to market faster.

    üéØ Higher Quality

    Continuous testing and feedback improve product quality.

    ü§ù Better Collaboration

    Cross-functional teams work together more effectively.

    üìà Increased Flexibility

    Adapt quickly to changing requirements and market conditions.

    üòä Greater Satisfaction

    Both customers and team members experience higher satisfaction.

    üîç Transparency

    Stakeholders have visibility into progress and challenges.

    Agile Methodologies

    Some of the Agile methodologies are as follows:

    • Extreme Programming (XP) - Focuses on engineering practices like pair programming, test-driven development, and continuous integration
    • Kanban - Visualizes workflow, limits work in progress, and focuses on continuous delivery
    • Lean - Emphasizes eliminating waste, amplifying learning, and delivering fast
    • Scrum - Scrum is the most popular Agile framework because of its simplicity, flexibility, teamwork focus, empirical process control, and widespread adoption.
    • Crystal - A family of methodologies that adapts to team size and project criticality
    • Feature-Driven Development (FDD) - Builds software feature by feature with short iterations
    • Dynamic Systems Development Method (DSDM) - Provides a comprehensive framework for Agile project management

    When to Use Agile

    Agile is particularly effective when:

    • Requirements are expected to change or evolve
    • Customer feedback is critical to success
    • Time to market is a competitive advantage
    • Innovation and experimentation are valued
    • Cross-functional collaboration is possible
    • The team can work in close proximity (physical or virtual)

    üéØ Scrum

    scrum definition
    Scrum (n): A framework within which people can address complex adaptive problems, while productively and creatively delivering products of the highest possible value.

    Origin and History

    Scrum was formally introduced in 1995 by Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland who co-created the framework. The term "Scrum" comes from rugby, where a scrum is a formation used to restart play, symbolizing teamwork and unified effort toward a common goal.

    üìö The Scrum Guide

    The official definition and rules of Scrum are maintained in "The Scrum Guide" by Schwaber and Sutherland, which is updated periodically to reflect lessons learned and best practices.

    Key Characteristics

    Scrum is:

    • Lightweight - Easy to understand and implement with minimal overhead
    • Simple to understand - Clear roles, events, and artifacts that are easy to learn
    • Difficult to master - Requires discipline, commitment, and cultural change to execute effectively

    Core Concepts

    üìÜ Sprint

    A time-box of one month or less during which a "Done", usable, and potentially releasable product increment is created.

    üóÉ Product Backlog

    An ordered list of everything that is known to be needed in the product, owned by the Product Owner.

    üóÇ Sprint Backlog

    The set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint, plus a plan for delivering the product increment.

    üì¶ Increment

    The sum of all Product Backlog items completed during a Sprint and all previous Sprints.

    Why Scrum Works

    • Empirical Process Control - Based on transparency, inspection, and adaptation
    • Self-Organization - Teams choose how best to accomplish their work
    • Collaboration - Emphasizes team interaction and shared responsibility
    • Value-Based Prioritization - Focus on delivering the highest value features first
    • Time-Boxing - Fixed-length iterations create rhythm and predictability
    • Iterative Development - Build incrementally and incorporate feedback continuously

    üß™ Scrum Theory

    theory empiricism

    Foundation: Empiricism

    Scrum is founded on empirical process control theory, or empiricism.

    Empiricism asserts that knowledge comes from experience and making decisions based on what is known.

    Empiricism has three key aspects:

    • Observation - Teams observe the actual results of their work
    • Experience - Learning comes from doing and reflecting on outcomes
    • Experimentation - Try new approaches and learn from results

    Defined vs. Empirical Process Control

    ‚ùå Defined Process Control

    • Plan everything upfront
    • Execute according to plan
    • Works for predictable, repeatable processes
    • Examples: Manufacturing, Construction
    • Problem: Software development is complex and unpredictable

    üß™ Empirical Process Control

    • Make a hypothesis (plan)
    • Execute and observe results
    • Inspect outcomes and adapt
    • Works for complex, unpredictable work
    • Advantage: Embraces change and uncertainty

    Approach

    Scrum employs an iterative, incremental approach to optimize predictability and control risk.

    üîÑ The Inspect and Adapt Cycle

    Scrum builds in frequent opportunities to inspect progress and adapt the plan:

    • Daily Scrum - Inspect daily progress toward Sprint Goal
    • Sprint Review - Inspect the product increment
    • Sprint Retrospective - Inspect the process and team dynamics
    • Sprint Planning - Adapt the plan based on what was learned

    This approach allows teams to:

    • Learn from experience and observations
    • Adapt to changing requirements
    • Make data-driven decisions
    • Continuously improve processes
    • Minimize risks through frequent inspection
    • Validate assumptions early and often
    • Respond quickly to feedback
    • Build the right product incrementally

    Complexity and Scrum

    Scrum acknowledges that software development is complex, not complicated:

    • Complicated - Many parts, but predictable (e.g., a car engine)
    • Complex - Unpredictable interactions, emergent behavior (e.g., software systems, human teams)

    For complex work, empiricism is the only way to make reliable predictions and effective decisions.

    üèõÔ∏è Scrum Pillars

    pillars foundation

    Scrum is built on three fundamental pillars that support empirical process control. These pillars are interdependent and reinforce each other:

    üîç Transparency

    Giving visibility to the significant aspects of the process to those responsible for the outcome.

    Everyone must have a shared understanding of the work and standards being used.

    üîé Inspection

    Timely checks on the progress toward a sprint goal to detect undesirable variances.

    Regular inspection prevents waste and identifies problems early.

    üîß Adaptation

    Adjusting a process as soon as possible to minimize any further deviation or issues.

    Teams must be empowered to make adjustments when needed.

    Deep Dive into Each Pillar

    üîç Transparency - Creating a Shared Reality

    Transparency means that all significant aspects of the process must be visible to those responsible for the outcome. This requires:

    • Common Language - All participants share the same understanding of terms
    • Definition of Done - Shared understanding of what "complete" means
    • Visible Artifacts - Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, and Increment are accessible to everyone
    • Open Communication - No hidden agendas or information hoarding

    ‚ö†Ô∏è Without Transparency:

    • Decisions are based on flawed or incomplete information
    • Problems remain hidden until they become critical
    • Trust erodes within the team and with stakeholders
    • Inspection and adaptation become ineffective

    ‚úÖ Practical Examples:

    • Daily stand-ups where everyone shares progress and impediments
    • Sprint boards visible to the entire team and stakeholders
    • Clear acceptance criteria for every user story
    • Burndown charts showing real-time progress

    üîé Inspection - Frequent Progress Checks

    Inspection requires that Scrum artifacts and progress toward agreed goals are inspected frequently and diligently to detect variances. Key points:

    • Frequent - Not so frequent as to impede work, but often enough to detect problems
    • Skilled Inspectors - Those performing the inspection must have the knowledge and skills
    • At the Point of Work - Inspection should happen where the work is being done
    • Non-Intrusive - Inspection should not get in the way of the work itself

    üìä Scrum's Inspection Opportunities:

    • Daily Scrum - Daily inspection of progress toward Sprint Goal
    • Sprint Review - Inspection of the product Increment
    • Sprint Retrospective - Inspection of the team's process and practices
    • Backlog Refinement - Ongoing inspection and refinement of backlog items

    üéØ What to Inspect:

    • Are we building the right product?
    • Are we meeting our Sprint Goal?
    • Are there any impediments blocking progress?
    • Is the quality of our work acceptable?
    • Are we working effectively as a team?

    üîß Adaptation - Responding to Change

    Adaptation means that if an inspector determines that aspects of a process deviate outside acceptable limits and the resulting product is unacceptable, adjustments must be made quickly. Key aspects:

    • Timely - Adaptations should be made as soon as possible to minimize deviation
    • Empowered Teams - Teams must have the authority to make changes
    • Evidence-Based - Adaptations should be based on inspection findings
    • Continuous - Adaptation is an ongoing process, not a one-time event

    üîÑ Types of Adaptation:

    • Product Adaptation - Adjusting what is being built based on feedback
    • Process Adaptation - Improving how the team works together
    • Technical Adaptation - Refining technical practices and tools
    • Planning Adaptation - Adjusting plans based on new information

    ‚öôÔ∏è Adaptation in Action:

    • Adjusting the Sprint Backlog during Daily Scrum
    • Revising the Product Backlog based on Sprint Review feedback
    • Implementing process improvements from Sprint Retrospective
    • Re-estimating work based on actual velocity

    The Pillar Relationship

    üîó Interdependence: The three pillars work together. Without transparency, inspection is flawed. Without inspection, adaptation is unfocused. Without adaptation, inspection is wasted.

    üíé Scrum Values

    values principles

    Scrum is supported by five core values that guide the team's work, actions, and behavior. When these values are embodied by the Scrum Team, the empirical pillars of transparency, inspection, and adaptation come to life:

    üöÄ COURAGE

    Scrum Team members have courage to do the right thing and work on tough problems.

    üéØ FOCUS

    Everyone focuses on the work of the Sprint and the goals of the Scrum Team.

    üèõÔ∏è COMMITMENT

    üîì OPENNESS

    Deep Dive into Each Value

    üöÄ Courage - Doing the Right Thing

    Courage enables team members to:

    • Tackle challenging and complex problems
    • Admit mistakes and learn from failures
    • Speak up when something isn't right
    • Challenge the status quo and suggest improvements
    • Deliver honest feedback, even when it's difficult
    • Push back on unrealistic expectations

    ‚úÖ Courage in Practice:

    • A developer admits they don't know how to solve a problem and asks for help
    • The team refuses to commit to a Sprint Goal they know is unrealistic
    • Someone raises a concern about product direction in Sprint Review
    • The team decides to refactor code even though it slows visible progress

    üéØ Focus - Concentrating on What Matters

    Focus means:

    • Concentrating on the Sprint Goal and Sprint Backlog
    • Minimizing work in progress (WIP)
    • Avoiding distractions and context switching
    • Prioritizing the most valuable work
    • Saying "no" to work outside the current Sprint
    • Protecting the team from interruptions

    ‚úÖ Focus in Practice:

    • The team declines a request to work on urgent bugs during a Sprint
    • Daily Scrums stay focused on the Sprint Goal
    • The team limits the number of items they pull into the Sprint
    • Dedicated time blocks for deep work without meetings

    üèõÔ∏è Commitment - Personal Accountability

    Commitment involves:

    • Committing to the Sprint Goal, not just individual tasks
    • Doing whatever it takes to help the team succeed
    • Following through on commitments
    • Supporting teammates when they need help
    • Being present and engaged in Scrum events
    • Continuously improving skills and practices

    üìå Important Note:

    Commitment is to the Sprint Goal and team success, NOT to completing every item in the Sprint Backlog. The team commits to doing their best, not to guaranteeing specific outcomes.

    ‚úÖ Commitment in Practice:

    • A developer helps test someone else's work to meet the Sprint Goal
    • The team stays late to resolve a critical impediment together
    • Everyone attends Sprint ceremonies prepared and engaged
    • Team members take ownership of problems, not just their tasks

    ü§ù Respect - Valuing Each Other

    Respect means:

    • Treating everyone as capable, independent professionals
    • Valuing different perspectives and expertise
    • Listening actively without interrupting
    • Assuming positive intent
    • Respecting decisions made by others in their role
    • Being considerate of others' time and commitments

    ‚úÖ Respect in Practice:

    • Product Owner respects the team's technical decisions
    • Developers respect the Product Owner's prioritization
    • Team members credit others for their contributions
    • Disagreements are handled professionally and constructively
    • Everyone's voice is heard in team discussions

    üîì Openness - Transparency in Action

    Openness includes:

    • Being honest about progress, challenges, and mistakes
    • Sharing information freely with the team and stakeholders
    • Being receptive to feedback and new ideas
    • Discussing problems openly rather than hiding them
    • Admitting when you don't know something
    • Making work visible to everyone

    ‚úÖ Openness in Practice:

    • Team openly discusses impediments in Daily Scrum
    • Sprint Review includes honest assessment of what wasn't completed
    • Retrospectives create safe space for candid discussion
    • Product Backlog is visible and accessible to all stakeholders
    • Team shares both successes and failures with stakeholders

    Living the Values

    üåü Key Insight: These values are not just nice-to-have ideals. They are essential for Scrum to work effectively. When team members embody these values, they create an environment where empirical process control thrives, leading to better products and happier teams.

    Values in Action - Daily Behaviors

    • In Daily Scrum: Show up on time (Respect), be honest about progress (Openness), help teammates (Commitment), stay focused on Sprint Goal (Focus), raise impediments (Courage)
    • In Sprint Planning: Challenge unrealistic expectations (Courage), commit to achievable goals (Commitment), respect estimates (Respect), share concerns openly (Openness), stay focused on value (Focus)
    • In Sprint Review: Demonstrate with confidence (Courage), listen to feedback (Respect), be honest about results (Openness), focus on value delivered (Focus), commit to improvements (Commitment)
    • In Sprint Retrospective: Share constructively (Openness), listen without defensiveness (Respect), identify improvements (Focus), implement changes (Commitment), address difficult topics (Courage)

    üìö The Six Key Processes

    The Scrum process consists of six interconnected activities that work together to create a potentially shippable product increment each Sprint:

    üìã 1. Product Backlog Creation & Management

    The Product Backlog is an ordered list of everything that is known to be needed in the product. It is the single source of requirements for any changes to be made to the product.

    üìù Contains:

    • User Stories - Features described from user perspective ("As a [user], I want [goal], so that [benefit]")
    • Business Value - Prioritized by value to customers and business impact
    • Acceptance Criteria - Clear definition of done for each item
    • Estimates - Relative sizing using story points or ideal days
    • Technical Debt - Items to improve code quality and architecture
    • Bugs - Defects that need fixing prioritized by severity
    • Spikes - Research or investigation work to reduce uncertainty

    üë§ Owner:

    Product Owner is responsible for content, prioritization, and accessibility

    üîÑ Evolution:

    Dynamic and constantly evolving based on feedback and changing needs

    üîÑ 2. Backlog Refinement (Grooming)

    The backlog is continuously refined and prioritized based on new insights, feedback, and changing business needs. This is an ongoing activity throughout the Sprint, typically consuming no more than 10% of Development Team capacity.

    üéØ Activities include:

    • Breaking down large items into smaller, manageable pieces that fit within a Sprint
    • Adding detail - estimates, acceptance criteria, and technical notes
    • Re-ordering items based on priority, dependencies, and risk
    • Removing items that are no longer relevant or valuable
    • Clarifying requirements with stakeholders and subject matter experts
    • Identifying dependencies and technical constraints

    üë• Participants:

    Product Owner, Development Team, sometimes Scrum Master

    ‚è±Ô∏è Frequency:

    Ongoing activity, often mid-Sprint refinement sessions

    üìÖ 3. Sprint Planning

    Sprint Planning initiates the Sprint by laying out the work to be performed. The entire Scrum Team collaborates to plan the Sprint and create a shared understanding of the Sprint Goal.

    ‚è±Ô∏è Time-box:

    Maximum 8 hours for a one-month Sprint (proportionally less for shorter Sprints)

    üë• Participants:

    Entire Scrum Team (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Development Team)

    ‚ùì The team answers three key questions:

    • Why is this Sprint valuable? ‚Üí Sprint Goal
    • What can be Done this Sprint? ‚Üí Sprint Backlog selection
    • How will the chosen work get done? ‚Üí Task breakdown & estimation

    üì§ Output:

    Sprint Goal + Sprint Backlog + Definition of Done + Plan for delivering the Increment

    üèÉ 4. Sprint Execution (The Heart of Scrum)

    The Sprint is a fixed time-box (typically 2-4 weeks) during which a "Done", usable, and potentially releasable product Increment is created. Sprints are the heartbeat of Scrum where ideas are turned into value.

    üéØ Sprint Characteristics:

    • Fixed Duration - Same length every time for predictability and rhythm
    • Sprint Goal - Clear objective that provides guidance and flexibility
    • No Harmful Changes - That endanger the Sprint Goal without team agreement
    • Quality Goals - Do not decrease; Definition of Done remains constant
    • Scope Clarification - Can be clarified and re-negotiated with Product Owner as more is learned
    • Cancellation - Only Product Owner can cancel if Sprint Goal becomes obsolete (rare)

    üó£Ô∏è Daily Scrum (Daily Stand-up):

    • Time-box: 15 minutes maximum, same time and place every day
    • Purpose: Inspect progress toward Sprint Goal and adapt Sprint Backlog as necessary
    • Format: Development Team synchronizes activities and creates plan for next 24 hours
    • Three Key Questions:
      • What did I do yesterday that helped meet the Sprint Goal?
      • What will I do today to help meet the Sprint Goal?
      • Do I see any impediment that prevents me or the team from meeting the Sprint Goal?
    • Not for problem solving - Issues identified are addressed immediately after with interested parties
    • Builds team commitment - Improves communication, identifies impediments, promotes quick decision-making

    üëÅÔ∏è 5. Sprint Review (Inspect the Product)

    The Sprint Review is held at the end of the Sprint to inspect the Increment and adapt the Product Backlog if needed. This is a working session, not just a presentation or demo. It's an informal meeting to foster collaboration.

    ‚è±Ô∏è Time-box:

    Maximum 4 hours for a one-month Sprint (proportionally less for shorter)

    üë• Attendees:

    Scrum Team + Key Stakeholders invited by Product Owner

    üìã Agenda includes:

    • What's Done: Product Owner explains what Product Backlog items are "Done" and not done
    • Demonstration: Development Team demonstrates the work and answers questions about the Increment
    • Discuss Backlog: Product Owner discusses current state of Product Backlog and projections
    • Collaborate: Entire group collaborates on what to do next based on the review
    • Review Context: Review of timeline, budget, potential capabilities, and marketplace changes
    • Next Steps: Discussion of next probable Product Backlog items and release dates

    üì§ Output:

    Revised Product Backlog + Probable items for next Sprint + Feedback incorporated

    üîç 6. Sprint Retrospective (Inspect the Process)

    The Sprint Retrospective is an opportunity for the Scrum Team to inspect itself and create a plan for improvements to be enacted during the next Sprint. It occurs after Sprint Review and before next Sprint Planning.

    ‚è±Ô∏è Time-box:

    Maximum 3 hours for a one-month Sprint (typically 45 min - 1.5 hours)

    üë• Attendees:

    Scrum Team only (safe, trusted environment for honest feedback)

    üéØ Purpose:

    Inspect how the last Sprint went regarding:

    • People: Team dynamics, collaboration, communication
    • Relationships: How team members interact and support each other
    • Process: Scrum practices, workflows, ceremonies
    • Tools: Technology, software, equipment used

    ‚ùì Key Questions:

    • What went well in the Sprint? (Continue doing)
    • What could be improved? (Start doing or stop doing)
    • What will we commit to improve in the next Sprint? (Action items)

    üì§ Output:

    Identified improvements + At least one high-priority process improvement committed for next Sprint

    üí° Key Insight

    These six processes work together in a continuous loop. Each Sprint is a mini-project with a goal, planning, execution, review, and improvement phase. The cycle repeats, with each iteration building on lessons learned from previous Sprints, creating a continuous improvement culture and delivering value incrementally.

    Key Concepts

    ‚õ≥ Sprint Goal

    An objective that provides guidance to the team on why they are building the Increment. It provides flexibility regarding functionality implemented within the Sprint.

    ‚úÖ Definition of Done

    A shared understanding of what it means for work to be complete. Ensures transparency and quality. All work must meet this definition before it's considered done.

    üì¶ Increment

    The sum of all Product Backlog items completed during a Sprint and all previous Sprints. The Increment must be in useable condition and meet the Definition of Done.

    üî• Velocity

    The amount of work a team completes in a Sprint, measured in story points. Used for forecasting and Sprint planning. Becomes more accurate over time.

    üîÅ Continuous Cycle: Scrum is not a one-time process. Each Sprint builds on the previous one, with continuous learning, adaptation, and improvement. The cycle repeats until the product is complete or the project ends.

    üìÜ Scrum Events (Ceremonies)

    events ceremonies
    Scrum prescribes four formal events for inspection and adaptation. These events are specifically designed to enable the transparency and inspection required. Failure to operate any events as prescribed results in lost opportunities to inspect and adapt.

    The Four Core Scrum Events

    üìã Sprint Planning

    When: Beginning of Sprint

    Duration: 4-8 hours

    Plan the work to be performed in the Sprint. Creates the Sprint Goal and Sprint Backlog.

    üó£Ô∏è Daily Standup

    When: Every day

    Duration: 15 minutes

    Inspect progress toward Sprint Goal and adapt Sprint Backlog as necessary.

    ‚úÖ Sprint Review

    When: End of Sprint

    Duration: 1.5-4 hours

    Inspect the Increment and adapt the Product Backlog. Demo to stakeholders.

    üîÑ Sprint Retrospective

    When: After Sprint Review

    Duration: 2-4 hours

    Inspect how the last Sprint went regarding people, relationships, process, and tools.

    Event Characteristics

    • Time-boxed - Each event has a maximum duration to ensure efficiency
    • Formal opportunities - Prescribed occasions for inspection and adaptation
    • Required attendance - Specific team members must participate in each event
    • Regular cadence - Events occur at predictable intervals
    • Focused purpose - Each event has specific goals and outcomes

    The Sprint as a Container

    The Sprint itself is considered an event that acts as a container for all other events. Each Sprint is a fixed time-box of one month or less, during which a "Done", useable, and potentially releasable product Increment is created.

    Sprints have consistent durations throughout a development effort. A new Sprint starts immediately after the conclusion of the previous Sprint.

    Benefits of Formal Events

    • üìä Transparency - Everyone knows when key activities occur
    • üîç Inspection - Regular opportunities to examine work and progress
    • üîß Adaptation - Timely adjustments based on inspection results
    • ‚è±Ô∏è Predictability - Minimizes need for ad-hoc meetings
    • üéØ Focus - Each event has a clear purpose and outcome
    • ü§ù Collaboration - Brings team together at right times

    ‚ö†Ô∏è Important

    Scrum Events are used to create regularity and to minimize the need for meetings not defined in Scrum. All events are time-boxed, meaning they have a maximum duration. Once a Sprint begins, its duration is fixed and cannot be shortened or lengthened.

    üìÜ Sprint Planning

    ceremony planning
    Sprint Planning initiates the Sprint by laying out the work to be performed for the Sprint. This resulting plan is created by the collaborative work of the entire Scrum Team.

    ‚è±Ô∏è Time Box

    • 2 weeks sprint: 4 hours maximum
    • 4 weeks sprint: 8 hours maximum

    üë• Attendees

    • Scrum Master (facilitates the meeting)
    • Product Owner (clarifies the details of the product backlog items)
    • Development Team (defines the work and effort necessary to meet the sprint commitment)

    üìÖ When

    Before starting with a new sprint - Sprint Planning happens at the beginning of every Sprint.

    üéØ Sprint Planning Addresses Three Key Questions

    ‚ùì WHAT can be delivered?

    The Development Team works to forecast the functionality that will be developed during the Sprint. The Product Owner discusses the objective that the Sprint should achieve and the Product Backlog items that would achieve the Sprint Goal.

    üîß HOW will the work get done?

    Having selected the Product Backlog items for the Sprint, the Development Team discusses how it will build this functionality into a "Done" product Increment. The work may be of varying size or estimated effort.

    üìù Key Inputs

    üìö Product Backlog

    Prioritized list of features and requirements

    üì¶ Current Product State

    Latest product increment and its capabilities

    üìä Current Product Velocity

    Historical team capacity and performance

    üöß Constraints

    Technical, business, or resource limitations

    üì§ Key Outputs

    ‚õ≥ Sprint Goal

    The Product Goal is the long-term objective for the Scrum Team. They must fulfill (or abandon) one objective before taking on the next.

    artifact sprint
    The Sprint Backlog is the set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint, plus a plan for delivering the product Increment and realizing the Sprint Goal. It is a forecast by the Development Team about what functionality will be in the next Increment and the work needed to deliver that functionality.

    üìç Key Characteristics

    üéØ Sprint-Specific

    Created during Sprint Planning. Contains only the work planned for the current Sprint. A new Sprint Backlog is created for each Sprint.

    üë• Team Owned

    Owned by the Development Team. Only the Development Team can change its Sprint Backlog during a Sprint. The Sprint Backlog is a highly visible, real-time picture of the work.

    üîÑ Flexible

    Emerges and evolves during the Sprint. As new work is required, the Development Team adds it to the Sprint Backlog. Work may be clarified and re-estimated.

    üìù What's in the Sprint Backlog?

    üìö Product Backlog Items

    Selected user stories, features, or work items from the Product Backlog that the team commits to completing in this Sprint.

    ‚úÖ Tasks

    Detailed tasks necessary to deliver the selected Product Backlog items. Tasks are created by decomposing the backlog items.

    An objective set for the Sprint that provides guidance to the team on why it is building the Increment.

    üõ†Ô∏è How Sprint Backlog is Created

    1. Sprint Planning Meeting - Development Team selects items from Product Backlog
    2. Team Discussion - Team discusses how to accomplish the work
    3. Task Breakdown - Items are decomposed into smaller tasks (often hours)
    4. Capacity Planning - Team ensures work fits within Sprint capacity
    5. Commitment - Team commits to delivering the selected items

    üìà Monitoring Progress

    üìâ Burndown Chart

    Visualizes remaining work over time during the Sprint. Shows if the team is on track to complete all committed work.

    üìä Task Board

    Visual representation of work items (To Do, In Progress, Done). Updated during Daily Standup to show real-time progress.

    15-minute daily meeting where team inspects progress and adapts the Sprint Backlog as necessary.

    Sprint Goal

    The Sprint Goal is an objective set for the Sprint that can be met through the implementation of the Product Backlog. It provides guidance to the Development Team on why it is building the Increment.

    The Sprint Goal gives the Development Team some flexibility regarding the functionality implemented within the Sprint. The selected Product Backlog items deliver one coherent function, which can be the Sprint Goal.

    Development Team Authority

    Only the Development Team can change its Sprint Backlog during a Sprint.

    • Add new tasks as they discover more work is needed
    • Remove tasks that are no longer necessary
    • Update task estimates as they learn more
    • Re-negotiate scope with Product Owner if Sprint Goal is at risk

    Best Practices

    • ‚úÖ Make it visible - Use a task board that everyone can see
    • ‚úÖ Update daily - Keep it current during Daily Scrum
    • ‚úÖ Break items into small tasks - Typically 1-8 hours each
    • ‚úÖ Track remaining work - Focus on what's left, not what's done
    • ‚úÖ Protect the Sprint Goal - Don't add unplanned work unless critical
    • ‚úÖ Be transparent - Make progress visible to all stakeholders

    üì¶ Increment

    artifact done
    The Increment is the sum of all the Product Backlog items completed during a Sprint and the value of the increments of all previous Sprints. At the end of a Sprint, the new Increment must be "Done", which means it must be in useable condition and meet the Scrum Team's Definition of Done.

    üìç Key Characteristics

    ‚úÖ "Done" and Useable

    The Increment must be in useable condition regardless of whether the Product Owner decides to release it. It meets the Scrum Team's Definition of Done.

    üì¶ Cumulative

    The Increment is cumulative - all previous Increments must be thoroughly tested, ensuring that all Increments work together.

    üöÄ Potentially Releasable

    Must be in a state where it could be released to users if the Product Owner chooses to do so. No additional work is required.

    ‚úÖ Definition of Done (Commitment)

    ‚úÖ Definition of Done (DoD)

    The Definition of Done is a formal description of the state of the Increment when it meets the quality measures required for the product.

    The moment a Product Backlog item meets the Definition of Done, an Increment is born. The Definition of Done creates transparency by providing everyone a shared understanding of what work was completed as part of the Increment.

    üìù What Might Be in Definition of Done?

    üìù Code Complete
    • Code reviewed
    • Merged to main branch
    • Follows coding standards
    üß™ Testing
    • Unit tests pass
    • Integration tests pass
    • Acceptance criteria met
    üìö Documentation
    • User documentation updated
    • Technical docs updated
    • Release notes prepared
    üöÄ Deployment Ready
    • Deployed to staging
    • Performance tested
    • Security reviewed

    üìà Building the Increment

    1. Sprint Planning - Team selects Product Backlog items for the Sprint
    2. Development Work - Team builds features according to acceptance criteria
    3. Continuous Integration - Code is integrated frequently (ideally daily)
    4. Testing - Work is tested against Definition of Done
    5. Sprint Review - Increment is demonstrated to stakeholders
    6. Potentially Release - Product Owner decides whether to release

    Multiple Increments per Sprint

    Multiple Increments may be created within a Sprint. The sum of the Increments is presented at the Sprint Review thus supporting empiricism. However, an Increment may be delivered to stakeholders prior to the end of the Sprint.

    Some teams practice Continuous Deployment, where Increments are released multiple times per day.

    Increment vs Sprint

    • üì¶ Increment is the artifact - the actual working product
    • ‚è±Ô∏è Sprint is the timebox - the container event during which work is done
    • ‚úÖ Each Sprint must produce at least one Increment
    • üöÄ The Increment must be "Done" and potentially releasable

    Why "Done" Matters

    • üîç Transparency - Everyone knows what "complete" means
    • üéØ Quality - Ensures consistent quality standards
    • ‚è±Ô∏è No Surprises - No hidden work or technical debt
    • üöÄ Releasability - Product Owner can release anytime
    • üìä Velocity Accuracy - Only "Done" work counts toward velocity
    • ü§ù Trust - Stakeholders trust the team's commitments

    ‚ö†Ô∏è Warning: "Almost Done" is Not Done

    If a Product Backlog item does not meet the Definition of Done, it cannot be released or even presented at the Sprint Review. Instead, it returns to the Product Backlog for future consideration.

    Best Practices

    • ‚úÖ Define "Done" clearly and make it visible to everyone
    • ‚úÖ Ensure Definition of Done is realistic and achievable within Sprint
    • ‚úÖ Include all necessary quality gates (testing, review, documentation)
    • ‚úÖ Evolve Definition of Done over time as team matures
    • ‚úÖ Never compromise on Definition of Done to "finish" work
    • ‚úÖ Automate as much as possible (CI/CD, testing, deployment)
    • ‚úÖ Get stakeholder agreement on Definition of Done

    üìñ Glossary

    terms definitions
    A comprehensive glossary of Agile and Scrum terminology to help you understand the key concepts, roles, artifacts, events, and practices in the Scrum framework.

    A

    ÔøΩ Acceptance Criteria
    Conditions that a software product must satisfy to be accepted by a user, customer, or other stakeholder. Defines when a user story is complete and working as expected.
    üîÑ Adapt
    One of the three pillars of Scrum (along with Transparency and Inspection). If an inspector determines that one or more aspects of a process deviate outside acceptable limits, adjustments must be made as soon as possible.
    üéØ Agile
    An iterative approach to project management and software development that helps teams deliver value faster with fewer headaches. Based on the Agile Manifesto and 12 principles.
    üìú Agile Manifesto
    A declaration of four key values and 12 principles to guide an iterative and people-centric approach to software development. Created in 2001 by 17 software developers.

    B

    üìö Backlog
    A list of work items (user stories, features, bugs) that need to be completed. Can refer to Product Backlog or Sprint Backlog.
    üìã Backlog Grooming (Refinement)
    The ongoing process of reviewing, prioritizing, and refining Product Backlog items to ensure they are ready for upcoming Sprints. Includes adding details, estimates, and ordering items.
    üìâ Burndown Chart
    A graphical representation of work left to do versus time. Shows remaining work in the Sprint (Sprint Burndown) or release (Release Burndown). Helps track progress toward Sprint Goal.
    üìà Burnup Chart
    A graphical representation showing completed work versus time. Opposite of a burndown chart, it tracks work completed rather than work remaining.

    C

    üîÑ Continuous Improvement (Kaizen)
    An ongoing effort to improve products, services, or processes through incremental improvements over time. Core principle in Agile and Scrum.
    üë• Cross-Functional Team
    A team with all the competencies needed to accomplish the work without depending on others outside the team. The Development Team in Scrum is cross-functional.

    D

    üîÑ Daily Scrum (Daily Standup)
    A 15-minute time-boxed event for the Development Team to synchronize activities and create a plan for the next 24 hours. Held at the same time and place each day. Team members answer three questions: What did I do yesterday? What will I do today? What's blocking me?
    ‚úÖ Definition of Done (DoD)
    A shared understanding of what it means for work to be complete. When a Product Backlog item meets the Definition of Done, an Increment is born. Ensures transparency and quality.
    ‚úÖ Definition of Ready
    Criteria that a user story must meet before being accepted into a Sprint during Sprint Planning. Ensures backlog items are clear, feasible, and testable.
    üë• Development Team
    Professionals who do the work of delivering a potentially releasable Increment of "Done" product at the end of each Sprint. Self-organizing and cross-functional. Optimal size is 3-9 members.

    E

    üß† Empiricism
    The theory that knowledge comes from experience and making decisions based on what is known. Scrum is founded on empiricism. It asserts that knowledge comes from experience and making decisions based on what is observed.
    ‚õ∞ Epic
    A large user story that cannot be delivered as defined within a single Sprint. Typically broken down into smaller user stories that can fit within a Sprint.

    I

    üöÄ Increment
    The sum of all Product Backlog items completed during a Sprint and the value of the increments of all previous Sprints. Must be in useable condition and meet the Definition of Done. Potentially releasable.
    üîç Inspect
    One of the three pillars of Scrum. Scrum users must frequently inspect Scrum artifacts and progress toward a Sprint Goal to detect undesirable variances.
    üîÑ Iteration
    A time-boxed period during which work is completed. In Scrum, a Sprint is an iteration. Iterations enable teams to deliver working software frequently.

    P

    üìö Product Backlog
    An ordered list of everything that is known to be needed in the product. The single source of requirements for any changes to be made. Owned and prioritized by the Product Owner.
    üìù Product Backlog Item (PBI)
    An item in the Product Backlog. Can be a user story, feature, enhancement, bug fix, technical debt item, or knowledge acquisition task.
    üéØ Product Goal
    The long-term objective for the Scrum Team. Describes a future state of the product and serves as a target to plan against. The Product Backlog emerges to define what will fulfill the Product Goal.
    üëë Product Owner (PO)
    The person responsible for maximizing the value of the product and managing the Product Backlog. Single person (not a committee) who represents stakeholders and makes final decisions on product direction.

    R

    üöÄ Release
    The transition of an increment from the development team to production where it can be used by customers. May occur at any time, potentially multiple times per Sprint.
    üìç Roadmap
    A plan of action for how a product or solution will evolve over time. Provides a strategic context for the Product Backlog and Sprint planning.

    S

    üéØ Scrum
    A lightweight framework for developing, delivering, and sustaining complex products. Consists of Scrum Teams and their associated roles, events, artifacts, and rules. Founded on empiricism and lean thinking.
    üë• Scrum Master (SM)
    A servant-leader for the Scrum Team. Helps everyone understand Scrum theory, practices, rules, and values. Removes impediments, facilitates events, and coaches the team and organization.
    üë• Scrum Team
    Consists of a Product Owner, Development Team, and Scrum Master. Self-organizing and cross-functional. Designed to optimize flexibility, creativity, and productivity.
    ü§ù Self-Organizing Team
    A team that chooses how best to accomplish their work, rather than being directed by others outside the team. Development Teams in Scrum are self-organizing.
    ‚è±Ô∏è Sprint
    A time-box of one month or less during which a "Done", useable, and potentially releasable product Increment is created. New Sprint starts immediately after the previous Sprint ends. Contains all Scrum events.
    üìã Sprint Backlog
    The set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint, plus a plan for delivering the product Increment and realizing the Sprint Goal. Owned by the Development Team.
    ‚õ≥ Sprint Goal
    An objective set for the Sprint that provides guidance on why the Increment is being built. Gives the Development Team flexibility regarding functionality implemented within the Sprint.
    üìã Sprint Planning
    A time-boxed event (4-8 hours) at the beginning of a Sprint where the Scrum Team plans the work to be performed. Creates the Sprint Goal and Sprint Backlog. Answers: What can be delivered? How will the work be done?
    üîÑ Sprint Retrospective
    A time-boxed event (2-4 hours) after Sprint Review where the Scrum Team inspects itself and creates a plan for improvements. Focuses on people, relationships, process, and tools. Answers: What went well? What could be improved? How can we improve?
    ‚úÖ Sprint Review
    A time-boxed event (1.5-4 hours) at the end of Sprint where the team inspects the Increment and adapts the Product Backlog. Demonstrates completed work to stakeholders and gathers feedback.
    üë• Stakeholder
    A person external to the Scrum Team with a specific interest in and knowledge of a product that is required for incremental discovery. Represented by the Product Owner and provides input during Sprint Review.
    üìä Story Points
    A unit of measure for expressing the overall effort required to fully implement a Product Backlog item or any other piece of work. Relative estimation rather than time-based.

    T

    ‚öôÔ∏è Technical Debt
    The implied cost of additional rework caused by choosing an easy solution now instead of using a better approach that would take longer. Should be tracked and addressed in the Product Backlog.
    ‚è∞ Time-box
    A fixed period of time during which an activity is conducted. All Scrum events are time-boxed. Sprint is a time-box of one month or less.
    üîç Transparency
    One of the three pillars of Scrum. Significant aspects of the process must be visible to those responsible for the outcome. Requires common standards so observers share a common understanding.

    U

    ÔøΩ User Story
    A short, simple description of a feature told from the perspective of the person who desires the new capability. Typically follows the format: "As a [type of user], I want [goal] so that [reason]."

    V

    üí™ Velocity
    The amount of Product Backlog work a Development Team can complete during a Sprint, measured in story points. Used for forecasting and capacity planning. Becomes more accurate over time.
